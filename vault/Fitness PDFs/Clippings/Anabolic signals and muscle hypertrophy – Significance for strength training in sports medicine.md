## Introduction

[Muscle hypertrophy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/muscle-hypertrophy), the increase in muscle mass and cross-sectional area due to the growth of muscle fibers, plays a pivotal role in [sports medicine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/sports-medicine). Especially in sports [orthopedics](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/orthopedics) and [traumatology](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/traumatology), optimal muscle function is critical not only for enhancing athletic performance but also for [injury](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/injury) prevention in terms of primary and secondary prophylaxis and for pre- and rehabilitation. The significance of muscle mass in the context of sports orthopedics and traumatology is underscored by epidemiological data indicating that [musculoskeletal injuries](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/musculoskeletal-injury) are among the most prevalent in sports, affecting millions of athletes annually and leading to substantial healthcare costs and lost training time.

A robust [musculoskeletal system](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/musculoskeletal-system) contributes to joint stability, injury resistance, and functional recovery. For instance, the prevalence of anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) injuries, which can result in prolonged rehabilitation and potential long-term disability, is closely associated with the strength and hypertrophy of surrounding musculature. Strengthening these muscles through targeted hypertrophy can significantly reduce the risk of injury and enhance post-injury recovery outcomes. However, our prospective multicenter [cohort study](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/cohort-analysis) has shown that only a small proportion of athletes who have undergone [ACL surgery](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/anterior-cruciate-ligament-reconstruction) meet the functional requirements for a return to sport at the end of medically prescribed rehabilitation and that a continuation of rehabilitation measures would be medically indicated in many cases.

Anabolic signals that promote hypertrophy include mechanical tension, metabolic stress, and muscle damage. These signals can be manipulated through various training regimens to maximize muscle growth. Mechanical tension, generated through resistance training, has been shown to be a primary driver of hypertrophy. However, metabolic stress, often induced by high-repetition, low-rest exercise protocols, also plays a crucial role by [stimulating growth factors](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/growth-promotor) and cellular swelling. Additionally, muscle damage resulting from [eccentric contractions](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/eccentric-muscle-contraction) may contribute to the remodeling and strengthening of muscle tissue.

Incorporating these anabolic signals into training regimens can be beneficial during the pre-injury conditioning (primary prophylaxis), prehabilitation and rehabilitation phases. The pre-injury conditioning aims to prepare the musculoskeletal system to withstand the demands of sports and reduce the incidence of injuries. Prehabilitation in sports medicine refers to the process of enhancing an athlete's physical fitness, strength, and muscle hypertrophy before an anticipated surgery or injury, to accelerate post-injury rehabilitation and improve recovery outcomes. In rehabilitation, post-injury hypertrophy-focused training can accelerate the recovery process, restore function, and prevent future injuries by addressing [muscle atrophy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/muscle-atrophy) that often accompanies immobilization and disuse.

The aim of this paper is to provide a comprehensive overview of the different anabolic signals that contribute to muscle hypertrophy and discuss how these signals can be strategically applied during training regimens in both prehabilitation and rehabilitation contexts. By understanding and leveraging these mechanisms, sports [orthopedists](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/orthopedist) and rehabilitation professionals can enhance the effectiveness of their interventions, ultimately improving outcomes for athletes.

## Muscle hypertrophy

Muscle hypertrophy, the increase in size of muscle cells, involves a series of cellular events initiated by anabolic signals that finally lead to an increased [protein synthesis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/protein-synthesis). This process is complex and involves multiple pathways and mechanisms. The initiation of muscle hypertrophy begins with an anabolic signal, which can be of mechanical (high muscle tension, muscle stretch) or metabolic nature (metabolic stress resulting from an accumulation of metabolites) or consists of the consequences of overloading the muscular structures (i.e., muscle damage). To elicit its effects, these signals must be sensed by the muscle cell and translated/transduced into the biological events that are associated with muscle growth, namely protein synthesis (signal transduction). The first part of the paper is intended to provide a brief overview of the [cellular processes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/cellular-processes) during hypertrophy, followed by an overview of the different anabolic signals in the second part ().

### Initiation of the anabolic signal

To elicit muscle hypertrophy by exercise, a stressor is needed to trigger the desired events within the muscle fibers that finally lead to an increased protein aggregation. Ultimately, the balance between protein-degrading and protein-building processes determines the direction and extent of the volume change. If protein aggregation prevails, muscle growth occurs. Depending on the exercise stimulus, mechanical or metabolic stress dominates the signal. Translated into training parameters, this means that exercises with high resistance and longer rest periods primarily lead to mechanical stress on the muscle fibers, whereas high repetition numbers with short breaks between sets cause greater metabolic stress. The latter can be accelerated by restricting the blood flow to the working muscles by inflatable cuffs, known as [Blood Flow Restriction Training](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/blood-flow-restriction-training) (BFR-Training), resulting in a distinct increase of the metabolic stress. If the applied load exceeds the capacity of the muscles, particularly in the context of mechanical stress (e.g. by high-load eccentric contractions), muscular damage occurs. However, we also assume that exhaustive exertion can cause damage due to metabolic stress, as discussed in a previous publication.

### Sensing the anabolic signal

Based on the outlined signal characteristics, the muscle cell needs suitable sensors that are capable to perceive the various signals and trigger the corresponding signaling cascades. The mechanosensors involved in the conversion of mechanical stimuli into biochemical signals that lead to muscle growth include [integrin](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/integrin) complexes (transmembrane proteins connecting the [extracellular matrix](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/extracellular-matrix) to the cytoskeleton), [titin](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/titin) (a giant protein spanning the length of the sarcomere), and stretch-activated ion channels (ion channels that open in response to stretch and allow ion influx).

In contrast to mechanical stress, metabolic stress does not usually induce muscle growth directly through the accumulated metabolites but through local and systemic changes triggered by metabolic by-products. This is why the term metaboreceptors is usually not used in this context. For example, it is assumed that the intramuscular accumulation of metabolites leads to cell swelling, which activates integrin-associated osmosensors that then trigger anabolic signaling cascades. Similarly, metabolites are known to provoke the release of [anabolic hormones](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/anabolic-steroid), such as testosterone, [human growth hormone](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/human-growth-hormone) and IGF-1, which exert their effect via the corresponding [hormone receptors](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/hormone-receptor). By contrast, [reactive oxygen species](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/reactive-oxygen-species), also assumed to be involved in muscle hypertrophy,, are not typically associated with specific receptors in the traditional sense, they interact with various cellular components and signaling molecules to mediate their effects.

It has been speculated that exercise-induced muscle damage (EIMD) has an anabolic effect in that the consequences of the damage induce cellular and systemic changes that lead not only to a repair but even to a build-up of muscle cells in terms of hypertrophy. That is, the damage to the muscle tissue and the resulting consequences acts as anabolic signals. These consequences include the inflammatory response (involving [neutrophils](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/neutrophil), macrophages, and the release of cytokines), Satellite [cell activation](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/cell-activation) (muscle stem cells that proliferate and either fuse with existing fibers or form new myofibers), hormonal changes and the expression of genes, related to muscle growth and remodeling.

### Signal transduction

Mechanical and metabolic stress during exercise trigger multiple signaling pathways that contribute to muscle hypertrophy. The activation of mechanosensors by exercise induced mechanical stress induces the activation of [focal adhesion kinase](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/focal-adhesion-kinase) (FAK) and subsequent stimulation of the [mTORC1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/reduced-nicotinamide-adenine-dinucleotide-dehydrogenase-ubiquinone) pathway. Metabolic stress, characterized by an increased AMP:ATP ratio, activates [AMPK](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/amp-activated-protein-kinase), which actually inhibits [mTORC1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/mammalian-target-of-rapamycin). In the long term, however, it indirectly supports protein synthesis through its positive effects on [mitochondrial biogenesis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/mitochondrial-biogenesis) and thus on energy availability, since protein synthesis requires a lot of energy (∼30% of the total ATP pool) to maintain anabolism. Furthermore, metabolic stress is associated with cell swelling, induced by [osmotic changes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/osmosis) during exercise, and activates [mTORC1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/reduced-nicotinamide-adenine-dinucleotide-dehydrogenase-ubiquinone) through [integrin](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/integrin) signaling and thereby increases protein synthesis. As mentioned above, exercise-induced muscle damage triggers an inflammatory response and satellite cell activation, promoting muscle repair and growth. Reactive oxygen species (ROS) generated during exercise act as signaling molecules, activating redox-sensitive pathways like [MAPK](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/mitogen-activated-protein-kinase), which regulate gene expression related to muscle growth and adaptation,. Finally, exercise has been reported to induce the release a plethora of cytokines that are synthesized and released by muscle cells, also known as myokines. These include, but are not limited to [myostatin](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/myostatin), myonectin, [irisin](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/irisin), [decorin](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/decorin), interleukin 6 and 15, [BDNF](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/brain-derived-neurotrophic-factor), and [FGF21](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/fgf21), which have been proposed to be involved in regulating [skeletal muscle](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/skeletal-muscle) mass and function.

The various stress signals, outlined above, converge on key pathways like mTORC1, [MAPK](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/mitogen-activated-protein-kinase), and calcium-dependent signaling to orchestrate the complex process of muscle hypertrophy. The mTOR complex 1 (mTORC1) is particularly important in this process, leading to the phosphorylation of downstream targets such as p70S6 kinase (p70S6K) and [ribosomal protein S6](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/protein-s6), which are crucial for initiating protein synthesis.

## Mechanical tension as an anabolic signal in muscle hypertrophy

Mechanical tension is an effective training stimulus for promoting muscle hypertrophy that has long been known to mankind. This signal is generated when the muscles contract against an external load, e.g., during resistance training. As outlined above, the mechanical stress is then converted into biochemical signals, ultimately leading to increased muscle [protein synthesis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/protein-synthesis), known as [mechanotransduction](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/mechanotransduction). Intensity, typically expressed as a percentage of one-repetition maximum (1RM), has traditionally been considered crucial for hypertrophy. Guidelines from the American College of Sports Medicine from 2009 pointed out that training intensities of at least 60%1RM would be required to achieve muscle hypertrophy (and strength gains) in healthy adults. For trained individuals, the lower end of the effective training intensity would be even higher. Recent research further highlights that not only the intensity but also the duration of mechanical tension is important. However, based on current research and meta-analyses, the recommendations for resistance training intensity to maximize hypertrophy are more flexible than previously thought. According to those data, low, moderate, and high loads (30–100% 1RM) can all stimulate similar hypertrophic adaptations when sets are performed to or near muscular failure. The reason for the effectiveness is probably that the lower mechanical stress is balanced out by an increasing metabolic stress, acting as an anabolic signal. Whatever the underlying mechanisms are, the observation that low intensities are also suitable for triggering muscle hypertrophy is a decisive paradigm shift that has important implications for the use of strength training in the field of pre- and rehabilitation. The mechanical resilience of the [musculoskeletal system](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/musculoskeletal-system) in patients is usually limited pre- and postoperatively, which means that a training program with high external loads is usually not an option. The fact that lower mechanical loads can also be effective opens up a broader spectrum of training therapy for clinicians and therapists.

## Metabolic stress as an anabolic signal in muscle hypertrophy

Metabolic stress, a crucial anabolic signal, significantly contributes to muscle hypertrophy. This type of stress is generated during resistance training, particularly through high-repetition, low-rest exercise protocols that lead to the accumulation of metabolites such as lactate, hydrogen ions, and inorganic phosphate. This accumulation of those metabolites triggers a cascade of cellular events promoting muscle growth. As outlined above, the mechanisms by which metabolic stress induces hypertrophy are multifaceted, including cellular swelling by an osmotic shift of water into the muscle fibers, ROS-production with its effect on mitogen-activated protein kinases (MAPKs), and probably the release of [anabolic hormones](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/anabolic-steroid).

Furthermore, the accumulation of metabolites, especially the accumulation of inorganic phosphate, is the primary driver for the onset of peripheral muscle fatigue. The high levels of fatigue and metabolite accumulation during intense exercise necessitate the activation of a broader spectrum of muscle fibers, leading to greater overall [muscle stimulation](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/muscle-excitation) and growth.

Recent studies emphasize the effectiveness of blood flow restriction (BFR) training, which leverages metabolic stress to maximize hypertrophy. BFR involves the application of cuffs or bands to restrict blood flow to the working muscles, enhancing metabolite accumulation and thereby amplifying the hypertrophic response even with lower exercise intensities. Of course, it must be taken into account here that the blood flow restriction caused by the cuffs not only creates a hypoxic environment for the working muscles, but is also accompanied by [venous pooling](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/venous-stasis), which itself could have an effect on muscle growth by enhancing muscle swelling.

Last but not least, it should be pointed out that metabolic stress can also lead to damage to the muscles and that the signals between mechanical and metabolic stress therefore overlap. For example, we were able to show that the additional application of BFR to a muscle damage protocol (downhill running) increased the indirect damage markers in the blood and described the potential mechanisms of metabolic muscle damage elsewhere. Interestingly, we were also able to show that metabolic preconditioning in the form of [ischemic preconditioning](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/ischemic-preconditioning) is able to reduce muscular damage caused by mechanical stress, which underlines the overlap between the two domains.

In conclusion, metabolic stress is a powerful anabolic signal that promotes muscle hypertrophy through mechanisms such as cellular swelling, reactive oxygen species (ROS) production, anabolic hormone secretion, and enhanced [muscle fiber recruitment](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/neurophysiological-recruitment). High-repetition, low-rest training protocols, and techniques like blood flow restriction effectively exploit metabolic stress to induce muscle growth. Understanding these mechanisms allows for the optimization of training regimens to maximize hypertrophy and improve athletic performance.

## Muscle damage as an anabolic signal in muscle hypertrophy

The initial phase of muscle damage involves the disruption of [sarcomeres](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/sarcomere) and the [sarcolemma](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/sarcolemma), leading to an inflammatory response characterized by the infiltration of [immune cells](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/immunocompetent-cell) such as [neutrophils](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/neutrophil) and macrophages into the damaged tissue. These immune cells help clear debris and secrete cytokines and growth factors that facilitate muscle repair. Exercise induced muscle damage has traditionally been viewed as a significant contributor to muscle hypertrophy. However, recent literature suggests that muscle damage might not be as crucial for hypertrophy as previously thought. While muscle damage can induce hypertrophic signaling, it is neither a necessary nor the most efficient pathway to muscle growth. Instead, it is increasingly recognized that mechanical tension and metabolic stress are of greater importance in promoting muscle hypertrophy without necessarily causing muscle damage. However, this connection should probably be presented differently: Muscle damage may not itself be necessary for the induction of muscle growth, but is merely the by-product of mechanical tension that exceeds the capacity of the muscles. However, if muscle tension is the targeted anabolic signal but muscle damage, usually associated with muscle soreness, never occurs after exercise, the mechanical tension during exercise is probably too far from the effective training stimulus.

In conclusion, while muscle damage may contribute to hypertrophy, it is not a necessary condition for muscle growth. Other mechanisms, such as mechanical tension and metabolic stress, play more central roles in promoting hypertrophy. Thus, training strategies that emphasize high-load eccentric contractions, to target high mechanical tension, may be effective to induce hypertrophy, but the observed muscle damage is not the primary driver of this adaptation. The initial increases in protein synthesis after such protocols likely reflect muscle repair and remodeling processes and should not be mistaken as hypertrophic responses. This notion is supported by the fact that the amount of muscle damage significantly decreases with chronic resistance training and poorly predicts protein synthesis and muscle hypertrophy. In practical applications, understanding that muscle damage is not essential for hypertrophy can serve as the basis of training protocols aimed at maximizing muscle growth while minimizing risk of [injury](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/injury) and re-injury. For example, focusing on progressive overload, varying exercise intensity, and incorporating both high-load and high-repetition training can optimize hypertrophy without the need for excessive muscle damage.

## Muscle stretch as an anabolic signal in muscle hypertrophy

Similar to high-intensity strength training, muscle stretching causes mechanical stress in the form of increased muscle tension. The difference, however, is that stretching is usually a passive tension in a stretched muscle position, whereas the tension in traditional strength training is created actively under ATP consumption through the contraction of the muscle fibers. The latter can therefore act isometrically in a specific joint angle or over the entire ROM. An [isometric contraction](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/muscle-isometric-contraction) in a stretched position is where the two forms overlap, if this is subsumed under stretching. The interesting fact however is that hypertrophy induced by stretching occurs independently of muscle contraction,, suggesting that passive stretching alone seems to be sufficient to stimulate muscle growth.

This is supported by an increasing body of research, revealing the potential muscle stretch as a complementary or alternative strategy to traditional resistance training. As in classic strength training, it is assumed that mechanical tension is the main trigger for muscle hypertrophy by activating various mechanosensitive signaling pathways leading to increased protein synthesis and muscle growth Stretching has been shown to increase the phosphorylation of p70S6K, a downstream target of [mTOR](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/mammalian-target-of-rapamycin), indicating enhanced anabolic signaling in [myotubes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/myotube) and rodents. At the cellular level, muscle stretch induces the addition of sarcomeres both in parallel and in series,. This adaptation not only increases muscle cross-sectional area but also potentially enhances muscle function and range of motion. Additionally, stretch-induced hypertrophy may involve the activation of satellite cells, which are crucial for muscle repair and growth.

The duration and intensity of stretching appear to be critical factors in eliciting hypertrophic responses. Studies in humans have shown that high-volume stretching protocols, typically involving durations of >30 min per session, are more effective in inducing muscle growth and strength gains. While the majority of research on stretch-induced hypertrophy has been conducted in animal models, human studies are beginning to corroborate these findings. However, the practical applicability of long-duration stretching protocols may be limited in some settings, as traditional resistance training appears to be more time-efficient for muscle growth and strength gains.

In conclusion, muscle stretch represents a promising anabolic signal capable of inducing significant hypertrophic and strength adaptations. Since passive stretching alone seems to be sufficient to stimulate muscle growth, this anabolic signal is of particular interest in situations where active resistance training is not feasible, such as during immobilization or in certain rehabilitation settings and makes it an intriguing area for further research.

## Role of nutrition in optimizing the hypertrophic response

Nutrition plays a crucial role in optimizing the hypertrophic response to the anabolic signals, with [protein intake](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/protein-intake) and timing being the most commonly discussed factors. Research has consistently shown that adequate protein consumption is essential for maximizing muscle protein synthesis (MPS) and promoting muscle growth. The current recommendation for individuals engaging in regular resistance training is a daily protein intake of 1.6–2.2 g/kg of body weight. Protein quality is also significant, with high-quality sources containing all [essential amino acids](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/essential-amino-acid), especially [leucine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/leucine), being more effective in stimulating MPS. Regarding timing, the concept of the “anabolic window” has been widely discussed, suggesting that protein consumption immediately post-exercise is crucial. However, recent evidence indicates that total daily protein intake may be more important than precise timing. Nevertheless, distributing protein intake evenly throughout the day in multiple meals appears to be an effective strategy for maximizing the hypertrophic response. Additionally, pre-sleep protein intake has shown promise in enhancing overnight MPS and overall muscle growth,. While protein is the primary macronutrient of interest, adequate carbohydrate and overall [energy intake](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/caloric-intake) are also important for supporting intense training and optimizing the anabolic environment,.

## Conclusion and practical application in pre- and rehabilitation

The strategic application of anabolic signals in prehabilitation and rehabilitation must be carefully tailored, as not all signals are suitable for these phases due to the reduced resilience of the musculoskeletal system. In prehabilitation, the focus is on the “better in, better out” principle, where the goal is to enhance muscular strength and mass to improve outcomes post-surgery. However, methods that induce high mechanical stress, like heavy resistance training, may not be appropriate due to the increased risk of injury. Instead, lower intensity methods that still promote hypertrophy, such as blood flow restriction training, can be more effective in this phase. Similarly, in rehabilitation, while muscle hypertrophy is crucial for restoring function, care must be taken to avoid excessive muscle damage, which can hinder recovery. Nutrition plays a pivotal role in both phases, with adequate [protein intake](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/protein-intake) essential for supporting muscle repair and growth. Timing of protein consumption, particularly around exercise sessions and before sleep, can further enhance the hypertrophic response, aiding in faster recovery and better overall outcomes.
